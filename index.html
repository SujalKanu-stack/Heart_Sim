How the Human Heart Works

🫀

The Heart as a Pump

The heart is a muscular organ roughly the size of your fist, beating 60–100 times per minute at rest — over 100,000 times a day. Its job is simple but vital: pump blood continuously around the body. It has four chambers: two atria (upper) and two ventricles (lower), separated by the cardiac septum.

🔵

Pulmonary Circulation

Deoxygenated blood (shown in blue) returning from the body enters the right atrium via the vena cava. It passes through the tricuspid valve into the right ventricle, which pumps it through the pulmonary artery to the lungs. There, CO₂ is exhaled and O₂ is absorbed, turning blue blood red.

🔴

Systemic Circulation

Freshly oxygenated blood (shown in red) returns from the lungs via the pulmonary veins into the left atrium. It passes through the mitral valve into the powerful left ventricle, which pumps it through the aorta to deliver oxygen and nutrients throughout the entire body.

🚪

Heart Valves

Four valves act as one-way doors, ensuring blood flows in only one direction. The tricuspid and mitral valves separate the atria from the ventricles (AV valves). The pulmonary and aortic valves (semilunar valves) guard the exits of the ventricles. The "lub-dub" heartbeat sound is produced by valves closing.

⚡

The Cardiac Cycle

Each heartbeat is one complete cardiac cycle. Systole is contraction (pumping phase); diastole is relaxation (filling phase). The cycle begins with both atria contracting simultaneously, followed by both ventricles contracting. The sinoatrial (SA) node — the heart's natural pacemaker — triggers each beat with an electrical signal.

📊

Heart Rate & Cardiac Output

Normal resting heart rate is 60–100 BPM. Athletes may have resting rates as low as 40 BPM due to a more efficient, stronger heart. Cardiac output (volume pumped per minute) = Heart Rate × Stroke Volume. During exercise, cardiac output can increase 4–5× to meet the body's increased oxygen demand.
